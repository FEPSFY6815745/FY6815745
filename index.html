Introduction

          The golden ratio, also known as the divine proportion, is a mathematical concept that has captivated the minds of scholars, artists, and nature enthusiasts for centuries. This ratio, 
          approximately 1.618, has been observed and admired in various elements of nature, from the delicate spirals of seashells to the intricate patterns of sunflowers (Mathai et al., 1974). 
          In this article, I will explore the appearances of the golden ratio in nature, examining its prevalence in different organisms and its role in creating visually appealing and harmonious 
          structures.

Discussion

          The golden ratio, denoted by the Greek letter φ (phi), is an irrational number expressed as, 1+52 or as a decimal 1.61803... This ratio can be derived from dividing a segment into two parts (A and B)
          such that A+BA = 1.618 (to 3d.p.) where A is greater than B (Akhtaruzzaman, 2011). To understand the golden ratio's presence in nature, one must first understand 
          its connection to the Fibonacci sequence. The Fibonacci series is a progression of numbers where each number is the sum of its two previous numbers in the sequence, Fn+2 = Fn+1+Fn. 
          As the sequence progresses, the ratio of each number to its predecessor approaches the golden ratio 1+52 ≃ Fn+1Fn (Beardon, 2009). 

          

          

          The golden ratio can also be used to find what’s known as the golden angle, g. When a circle gets split into two arcs following the golden ratio, the 
          golden angle is the smaller of the two angles created by these arcs, which is ~137.5° (Huang et al., 2023). It can additionally be determined by taking 
          an example with packing elements - meaning the placement of each element is optimised to pack together as densely as possible within a figure - such as 
          seeds in the sunflower head (Fig1) The spiralling pattern of sunflower seeds follows the Fibonacci sequence, allowing each seed to maximize its 
          exposure to sunlight (Mathai et al., 1974). As the number of elements tends to infinity, the arc length converges to 
          1φ2 = 3-52 = 0.382 (to 3d.p.) FIg2 (Huang et al. , 2023).

          The golden angle is also found within the veins of insect wings. In an investigative study conducted by Huang, X. and his team in 2023, they discovered 
          that the golden angle features prominently in the wing structure of dragonflies.This angle is not randomly placed; rather, it is strategically 
          located at the trailing edges and tips of the wings. This particular location of the golden angle leads to an optimized arrangement of veins, which in 
          turn, significantly enhances the biomechanical properties of the wing (Huang et al., 2023).
