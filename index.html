Abstract

        This ratio and the sequences it generates have been found to be prevalent in many natural systems, including the distribution if veins in an insect, the arrangement of seeds in a sunflower, and even the proportions 
        of the human body. Due to the golden ratios popularity, the ratio has often been overrepresented in examples such as the nautilus shell.

Introduction

          The golden ratio, also known as the divine proportion, is a mathematical concept that has captivated the minds of scholars, artists, and nature enthusiasts for centuries. This ratio, approximately 1.618, has been 
          observed and admired in various elements of nature, from the delicate spirals of seashells to the intricate patterns of sunflowers (Mathai et al., 1974). In this article, I will explore the appearances
          of the golden ratio in nature, examining its prevalence in different organisms and its role in creating visually appealing and harmonious structures.

Discussion

          

          The golden ratio, denoted by the Greek letter φ (phi), is an irrational number expressed as, 1+52 or as a decimal 1.61803... 
          This unique ratio can be derived from dividing a segment into two parts (A and B) such that 
          A+BA = 1.618 (to 3d.p.) under the condition that A is greater than B (Akhtaruzzaman, 2011). To understand 
          the golden ratio's presence in nature, it is important to first understand its inherent connection to the Fibonacci sequence. The Fibonacci series is a progression of numbers where each number is the sum of its two previous numbers in the sequence, shown by: 
          Fn+2 = Fn+1+Fn. As the sequence progresses, the ratio of each number to its predecessor approaches the golden ratio 1+52 ≃ Fn+1Fn (Beardon, 2009).

          The golden ratio can also be used to find what’s known as the golden angle, g. When a circle gets split into two arcs following the golden ratio, the 
          golden angle is the smaller of the two angles created by these arcs, which is ~137.5° (Huang et al., 2023). It can additionally be determined by taking 
          an example with packing elements - meaning the placement of each element is optimised to pack together as densely as possible within a figure - such as 
          seeds in the sunflower head. The spiralling pattern of sunflower seeds follows the Fibonacci sequence, allowing each seed to maximize its 
          exposure to sunlight and therefore grow efficiently (Mathai et al., 1974). As the number of elements tends to infinity, the arc length converges to 
          1φ2 = 3-52 = 0.382 (to 3d.p.) Fig2 (Huang et al. , 2023).

          The aforementioned golden angle is also found within the veins of insect wings. In an investigative study conducted by Huang, X. and his team in 2023, it was 
          discovered that the golden angle features prominently in the wing structure of dragonflies. This angle isn't randomly placed. Instead, it is strategically 
          located at the trailing edges and tips of the wings, this particular position of the golden angle leads to an optimized arrangement of veins, which in 
          turn, significantly enhances the biomechanical properties of the wing (Huang et al., 2023).

          Nautilus shells are frequently linked to the golden ratio, making them a notable symbol of this mathematical concept. However, the belief that these shells perfectly align with the golden ratio has been
          proven inaccurate. Research has found that these shells have an aspect ratio of 1.310 across the Nautilus genus (Bartlett, 2019). Additionally, other studies have reported findings of an aspect 
          ratio of 1.33, which is a ratio of 4:3 (Fablo, 2005). Neither of these ratios are equivalent to the golden ratio, which is approximately 1.618… As shown by the spirals below, the nautilus shell 
          fits the 4:3 graph closer than the golden ratio.

          The golden ratio has been associated with the proportions of the human body, though this holds relevance as an aesthetic observation rather than something 
          functional i.e. structural optimisation. According to this article (Ulmer, et al., 2009) the golden ratio can be found in “the perfect body” 
          by measuring distances between certain body parts such as the nose and the mouth and eyes and chin, though every persons body is different so when an 
          average is taken over a population, the ratio between proportions gets closer to the golden ratio (Golden ratio in Human Body, 2022). 
          Most famously, Da Vinci's Vitruvian man has long been associated with the divine proportion as the drawing “represents the art and science of human body 
          relationships and proportions” (Alzyoud, et al., 2021) and while it does exhibit some dimensions that approximate the golden ratio - 
          the diagram suggests that the upper body should be about 3.75 times the size of the lower body for instance - it does not perfectly align with the 1.618 
          ratio.
